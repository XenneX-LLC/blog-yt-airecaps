Summer has a specific relationship with YouTube. More downtime means more screen time, and YouTube is where that time goes. The problem isn't the platform — it's that autoplay and recommendations are optimized for session length, not for your interests. The result is a lost afternoon where you watched six things you didn't plan to and none of the things you actually wanted to see.

Building a deliberate watch list before summer is how you fix this.

## The Algorithm Is Not Your Friend (In This Context)

YouTube's recommendation engine is very good at one thing: showing you something you'll click on. That's different from showing you something you'll value. The session-length optimization means it tends toward topics you've recently engaged with, content that provokes a reaction, and videos just outside your comfort zone with high click-through rates.

This is useful when you don't know what you want. It's counterproductive when you do. Summer is when most people have a sense of what they'd like to spend time on — a creator they've been meaning to catch up on, a topic they've been curious about, a format they enjoy but don't prioritize during busier months.

## Building the Queue Intentionally

The move is to build your watch list before you open the app. Not during a browsing session when the algorithm is already steering you — before, from a cold start.

**Start with creators, not topics.** YouTube's search is topic-based, which returns whatever ranks highest for that topic. Channel-first thinking is different: you're asking "what has this creator I trust made recently?" rather than "what's popular about this topic right now?" The output is higher-quality and more aligned with your actual taste.

**Use Watch Later as a staging area, not a graveyard.** Most Watch Later lists are where videos go to die. The functional version is to use it as a deliberate queue — add things with intent, then actually work through the list before adding more. If your Watch Later list is over 50 videos, it's not a queue, it's aspirational hoarding.

**Batch by creator, not by topic.** A session watching three videos from one creator is different from watching three videos about the same topic from three different creators. The creator-batch session gives you context, continuity, and a sense of how someone thinks rather than a surface-level sampling across multiple sources.

**Set a time boundary before you start.** Decide before you open YouTube how long you're going to watch. Not a "I should stop soon" soft limit — an actual time. This is the difference between intentional consumption and a session that ends when you finally get bored enough to stop.
