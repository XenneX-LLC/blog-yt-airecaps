Eight days from now, Apple drops the keynote. Then they drop everything else.

The keynote is about 2.5 hours. Fine. Most people can handle 2.5 hours if they care about the topic. The problem is what comes after: 200+ session videos, platform state of the union talks, design panels, developer deep dives. By June 9, there will be more WWDC content than any person can watch in a month.

Here's what that actually looks like, and how to handle it without losing a week.

## What Apple Drops on June 8

The keynote is the public-facing piece — what Apple wants consumers to know. It covers the big announcements in polished, marketing-approved form. Worth watching if you have 2.5 hours. Worth AI Recapping if you don't.

After the keynote, the developer-facing content starts. Platforms State of the Union is the one worth flagging: it's where Apple goes technical and tells developers what actually changed under the hood. Less polished than the keynote, more substantive. Usually around 90 minutes.

Then come the sessions — short, focused deep dives on specific APIs, frameworks, and features. There are usually 150-200 of them. This is where the real WWDC content lives. It's also where most people give up.

## The Watch List Problem

Every year, the same thing happens: people watch the keynote, open the WWDC app, see 200 sessions, and immediately feel behind. They add 40 to their watch list. They watch three. By June 15 the whole thing feels like an obligation.

The useful reframe: you don't need to watch the sessions. You need to know what's in them.

If you're an iOS developer, you need to know which APIs changed, what's deprecated, and what the new patterns look like. You don't need to watch a 35-minute session on SwiftUI animations at 1x speed to know that. A good summary tells you whether it's relevant to your work in 3 minutes.

If you're not a developer, the sessions are mostly not for you anyway. What you want is a clear map of what changed and why it matters — which the keynote gives you in broad strokes and AI Recaps can give you in specific ones.

## The AI Recaps Strategy for WWDC Week

Start with the keynote. Watch it or recap it — either is fine. Get the headline announcements clear in your head.

Then identify what actually matters to you. What platforms do you build on? What products do you use daily? What's your one area of genuine Apple curiosity this year? That's your watch list — not "everything that looks interesting," but the two or three things that are genuinely relevant to your work or life.

For everything else: let AI Recaps handle it. Drop the session video, get a summary, decide in 3 minutes whether it's worth a full watch. Most of the time it won't be. That's fine — now you know that in 3 minutes instead of 35.

By June 9, you'll know what changed, why it matters, and exactly which sessions are worth your full attention. Everyone else will be adding things to their watch list they'll never open.

WWDC is 8 days away. Plan the recap strategy now, not after you're staring at 200 videos wondering where to start.
