Somewhere between now and July 19, there are going to be 104 World Cup matches. You are not going to watch 104 World Cup matches. Nobody is watching 104 World Cup matches.

The question isn't whether to watch everything — it's how to stay informed enough to have real opinions when it matters, without the World Cup becoming a second job.

## The Honest Breakdown of World Cup Viewership

There are a few different kinds of World Cup followers. There are the people who set alarms for 6am group stage matches and watch every minute of every game with the focus of someone taking a professional exam. These people exist. They are not most people.

Most World Cup engagement looks like this: you'll watch the US games if you're American. You'll watch the matches that are on when you happen to be at a bar or a friend's place. You'll follow the tournament results the way you follow election results — not by watching every vote get counted, but by checking in at key moments and relying on summary information the rest of the time.

This is not a lesser form of fandom. It's just how most people engage with a tournament that runs for six weeks and produces more content than any reasonable person can consume.

## What You Actually Need to Sound Informed

You need to know the early results in each group — who's winning, who's already out, who's the dark horse people are talking about. You need to know one or two story lines from the tournament so far (an unexpected loss, a breakout player, a controversial call). You need to know when the US is playing. The rest is optional.

If you have those five things, you can participate in every World Cup conversation for the next six weeks. You don't need to have watched the Iran vs. Japan group stage match in full. You need to know what happened.
