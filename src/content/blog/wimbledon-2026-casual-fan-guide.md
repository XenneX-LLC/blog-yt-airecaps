Wimbledon starts June 29. Two weeks of grass court tennis, the most ceremonially British sporting event in the world, and a schedule that is completely incompatible with having a job in North America.

The matches start in the morning UK time, which means they're running from roughly 7am–2pm Eastern — right through the workday. A Djokovic first-round match goes four hours sometimes. Alcaraz is serving at 250 km/h and it still turns into a five-set marathon. You cannot live-watch all of it. You shouldn't try.

Here's how to actually follow Wimbledon this year.

## Know the Names Before Round 1

Wimbledon rewards casual fans who did a small amount of homework. The draw is 128 players per gender; you need to know maybe six.

**Men's to watch:** Carlos Alcaraz is the defending champion and the most watchable player in tennis right now — high-variance, huge shots, emotional. Novak Djokovic, still. Jannik Sinner if he's healthy. Those three will cover 90% of the meaningful storylines.

**Women's to watch:** Iga Świątek is the world number one but has historically been inconsistent on grass. Aryna Sabalenka has the power game for the surface. Elena Rybakina won Wimbledon in 2022 and is built for it. Any of these three making or missing the semifinals generates a week of content.

Knowing these names means you can follow the bracket without researching it and understand the stakes of any given match someone mentions.

## Which Rounds Actually Matter

For the casual fan, Wimbledon has essentially three phases:

**Week 1 (June 29 – July 5):** First and second rounds. Upsets happen here. Check in on the upset alerts, ignore everything else. This is when you want highlights, not live watching.

**Week 2 starts July 7:** Round of 16 onward. This is when the field gets interesting. If you're going to catch any matches live, this is the window — the schedule is more predictable and the stakes are clear.

**Semifinals and Final (July 11–13):** These are the matches worth blocking time for. The men's final in particular tends to run long and deliver. Clear your Sunday afternoon for the 13th if you can.

## How to Stay in the Conversation Without Watching Everything

Wimbledon is a conversation sport. People talk about it at work, post about it, share clips. You can participate fully without watching six hours a day.

**Morning score check.** A two-minute look at overnight results each morning keeps you current. You know who survived, who went out, what to expect today.

**One highlight per round.** The key points and match highlights from major matches run 6–10 minutes. Watching the final set tiebreak of an Alcaraz match is a better use of 15 minutes than watching the first two hours live.

**Follow the bracket, not the schedule.** The bracket tells you what matters next. The schedule tells you when it's on. Focus on the bracket and catch up on results — the schedule is for people who can watch live.
