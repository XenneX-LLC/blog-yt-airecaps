Game 1 tips off tonight. The Knicks are in the Finals for the first time since 1999, and every sports media outlet has been in full production mode since the conference finals ended.

Here's what that looks like in practice: one game tonight generates roughly 4–6 hours of secondary content. The pre-game show. The post-game panel. The overnight film breakdown. The morning reaction podcasts. The newsletter roundups. The YouTube film room videos that drop 18 hours later.

Most of that content is the same information processed in different formats. Here's how to actually follow the series without it eating your week.

## What's Actually Worth Your Time

**The game itself.** This is the non-negotiable. If you're going to follow the Finals, watch at least some live basketball. Even 20 minutes of actual play gives you the context to make sense of everything else.

**One good postgame take.** Not four. Not a panel of six people. One writer or analyst whose frame you trust, read or watched after each game. The best postgame analysis is usually published within 2 hours of the final buzzer — after that, you're getting takes on takes.

**The injury report and lineup news.** This is the thing that actually changes game-to-game analysis. Five minutes in the morning the day of each game is worth more than an hour of pregame panel speculation.

## What's Pure Volume

**The pregame show.** By the time tip-off happens, 90% of what the pregame panel said will be irrelevant. They're filling time. The useful part is the final 10 minutes when they cover the actual game plan and matchups — everything before that is atmosphere.

**Podcast reaction episodes.** The first 30 minutes of any reaction podcast are usually the most useful. The full runtime — often 90 minutes to 2 hours for a Finals game — is almost never worth it unless you have commute time to fill. Speed it up to 1.5x at minimum.

**Film breakdowns.** These are genuinely good. They're also 25–40 minutes each, and there will be multiple per game. You probably can't watch all of them and still function. Pick one per game, from a source that focuses on the matchup you actually find interesting.

## Where AI Recaps Fits

The format that benefits most from recapping is the long-form stuff — the 90-minute podcast episode, the 3-hour radio show, the multi-part YouTube series. These have real signal buried in a lot of runtime.

Drop the audio or video into [AI Recaps](https://airecaps.com), get the key points in 3–5 minutes, decide whether it's worth a full listen. Most of the time: no, but now you know that in 3 minutes instead of finding out at the 45-minute mark.

The Finals run through mid-June. That's a lot of content. The triage system matters more over a series than for a single game.

Game 1 is tonight. Watch the game. Read one good take after. Sleep at a reasonable hour.

Everything else can wait until morning.
