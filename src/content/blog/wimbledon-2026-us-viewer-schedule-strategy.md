Wimbledon opens June 30. For US viewers, there's a structural problem with following it: the matches run from roughly 7am–2pm Eastern time, with Centre Court play starting at noon London time. If you want to watch live, you're watching before most people's workday starts. If you sleep in, you wake up to results you didn't ask for.

Neither extreme — setting a 6am alarm every day for two weeks, or avoiding results while watching highlights at night — actually fits how most people live. Here's the approach that does.

## Understand the Schedule Before You Commit to Anything

Wimbledon runs June 30 through July 13. The draw drops Friday June 27 — that's when you find out which side of the bracket Alcaraz, Djokovic, and Sinner are on, and which quarterfinal matchups are likely. Before the draw, everything is theoretical.

After the draw, spend 10 minutes building a short list: three or four matches worth actually watching live in the first week, based on the players involved and the stakes. Don't try to watch everything. Pick the matches that actually interest you and plan around those specifically.

The rest of the tournament you follow through results and highlights.

## The Realistic Viewing Tiers

**Tier 1 — Watch live:** The semifinals (July 11) and the men's final (July 13). These are genuinely worth structuring around. The men's final in particular runs in the early afternoon ET on a Sunday — reasonable timing, and worth blocking. Set a reminder when the draw is out and you know who's in it.

**Tier 2 — Catch same-day:** Any quarterfinal involving a player you care about. These run during Week 2, which starts July 7. The matches are important enough that highlights don't fully replace being relatively current — but you don't need to watch live. A same-day watch on replay in the evening works.

**Tier 3 — Highlights only:** Everything in Week 1. Rounds 1 through 3 are where upsets happen and the field narrows. You want to know what happened but you don't need to watch it. A 10-minute highlights package per day covers it.

## One More Thing Before the Draw

The Wimbledon women's draw is worth paying attention to this year. Iga Świątek on grass is historically volatile — she's the best player in the world on clay and hard courts and has never won Wimbledon. Every year the question is whether this is the one. Sabalenka and Rybakina both have the power game for grass. The women's bracket tends to be less predictable than the men's and the results are often more interesting.

Most US tennis coverage leans heavy on the men's draw. Worth building in at least one women's match to your Tier 2 list when the draw drops Friday.
